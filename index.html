Introduction

Human rights are an important part of our society, and the right to health is no different.

Medicinal drug shortages are a huge problem around the world for all kinds of people.
Some people even need it to live, and recently there was a huge shortage of insulin in the US.
Around 1 in 9 people living in the US have some form of diabetes, and rely on insulin everyday to regulate their blood glucose levels.


So, how are people dealing with it? Why is there a shortage in the first place?
More importantly, how is it challenging peoples' rights?

Sub-Question #1

Who is being affected by the shortage and how are they dealing with it?

Obviously the shortage affected US citizens, but what demographics were affected by it? Were some affected more than others?
What did these people do about it?

There are around 341.8 million people living in the US, and about 38.4 million of them are diabetics.
That's almost 3 times as many people as the population of Tokyo, which is the most populated city in the world.
It also seems West Virginia has the most diabetic people in the US, while South Dakota and Colorado have the least.

A lot of diabetics had to call around nearby pharmacies to try and find some insulin, but most of the time with no success.
This, of course, caused a lot of stress and anxiety for people, especially those that had almost run out of insulin.
Some people had tried to ration what little insulin they had left, but even that was only a temporary solution.
Professionals advised people to try and get their insulin week earlier, and if they couldn't get it then they should "seek emergency care".
As you can expect, some people tried any alternatives they could find, such as switching to prefilled injectable pens, or using different types of insulin
A lot of the time however, this doesn't really work very well. Everyone has different reactions to different types of insulin, so you can't really use other types.

Sub-Question #2

What caused the shortage and what insulin was being affected?

Different types of insulin are produced differently, so what kinds are being affected? How many people use each one?
What's actually causing the shortage?

Eli Lilly is the manufacturer of the most common type of insulin used, known as Humalog.
Humalog is the main type of insulin being affected by the shortage, with prices being reported as high as $274.70 for a single 10ml.
On top of that, Danish insulin manufacturer Novo Nordisk have started phasing out another well-known type of insulin, Levemir.
They plan to have it gone completely by the end of 2024, mainly due to "global manufacturing issues" and "decreasing insurance coverage".

Medicinal drug shortages can be caused by a variety of things, including unpredictable demand and manufacturing problems, as is the case with Humalog.
Humalog is apparently having shortages due to a "brief delay in manufacturing", causing pharmacies to be out of stock.
As previously mentioned, this is rising insulin prices drastically, and Eli Lilly has been putting in systems to cap it at $35, making it more afforable for everyone.

Insulin supplies are normally decently predictable, so when something like this happens suddenly it gets a little concerning.
Another thing to note is that typically, sterile injectables are at higher risk of shortage than other drugs, which does include insulin.

Sub-Question #3

How are people's rights being affected by the shortage?

The right to food and health is an important one, and considering the fact that insulin is a medicine that many people rely on to live, how is the shortage affecting it?

Article 25 of the Universal Decleration of Human Rights states that everyone has a right to food and health.
Considering the fact that diabetics need insulin to both eat food and maintain their blood glucose levels, it seems to go against this right.
When someones blood glucose gets too high it can have serious health implications, sometimes if it goes too high it can even be fatal.
